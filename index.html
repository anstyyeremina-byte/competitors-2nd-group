Market Profile — United Kingdom
🇬🇧

United Kingdom

The UK is the strongest benchmark for native Open Banking payment logic, especially for VRP. VRPs are formally part of the UK Open Banking framework and were first implemented for sweeping by the CMA9 banks. The market has since moved toward commercial VRP as well.

CMA9 + VRP Mandate
Ba
Barclays
Large incumbent · ASPSP + API Provider
VRP ✓ BNPL ✗ Bulk ~
OB/OF Positioning
Large incumbent bank with a formal Open Banking API layer. Developer stack clearly includes Payment Initiation and Variable Recurring Payment APIs — a strong infrastructure benchmark.
Target Segment
Mainly TPPs, fintechs, payment providers and API-led partner use cases. BNPL-like signal sits more in partner finance and point-of-sale finance.
Service Role
Primarily ASPSP + account provider + API provider. In financing scenarios, also acts as a lender through partner finance.
VRP API Layer Bulk
Strengths
Regulated Open Banking infrastructure, especially payment initiation and formal VRP capability. Makes Barclays useful when a case needs a benchmark for how a traditional bank exposes Open Banking payment functionality through APIs.
Limitations
Less convincing for embedded BNPL UX or a strongly packaged bulk/business payment proposition. Its BNPL-like offer is closer to traditional point-of-sale finance. Strongest public signal is infrastructure rather than a broad product model.
Key Conclusion
Best for bank-led Open Banking compliance, API enablement and formal VRP availability — not the strongest UK reference for customer-facing BNPL or business payment orchestration.
Na
NatWest
Incumbent · ASPSP + OB Payment Platform (Payit)
VRP ✓ BNPL ~ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Stronger bank-led Open Banking product benchmark — packages Open Banking into a visible service layer through Payit. Publicly positions VRP as part of the future of payments.
Target Segment
Merchants, businesses, corporates and recurring billers, while still serving end users through merchant-side payment flows. Business tooling targets payroll, supplier payments and finance operations.
Service Role
ASPSP + account provider, Open Banking payment platform provider through Payit, and also a business payments provider through Bankline, Bacs and related bulk-payment tools.
VRP / Payit Bacs / Bulk Business API
Strengths
Strongest in VRP / recurring payments and bulk/business payments. Payit is explicitly positioned as an Open Banking payment solution. NatWest has publicly backed VRP as a business and consumer payment option. Business stack supports bulk payments, Bacs, file upload, host-to-host automation and a newer global bulk-payment solution.
Limitations
Weaker as a BNPL benchmark — instalment logic looks closer to traditional lending/instalment credit than a modern embedded BNPL ecosystem. Some bulk-payment experiences differ by channel, with lighter functionality in standard online banking.
Key Conclusion
The strongest UK benchmark if your case is about native VRP logic, recurring collections, merchant Open Banking payments and business payment infrastructure. Much closer to a real service model than a pure compliance/API benchmark.
🇬🇧
UK Country Conclusion

The UK remains the strongest benchmark for native VRP logic. Within this market, NatWest is the more relevant benchmark for your case — it combines Open Banking payments, recurring payment use cases and business payment infrastructure. Barclays is still useful, but mainly as a benchmark for regulated Open Banking API infrastructure. For BNPL, neither bank is the strongest benchmark — both look closer to traditional lending / instalment finance than to embedded BNPL.

Market Profile — Netherlands
🇳🇱

Netherlands

The Netherlands operates under PSD2 / Open Banking with iDEAL as the dominant payment rail. A strong benchmark for bank-side bulk and business payment flows. Note: Rabobank is the bank while Yapily is an open banking provider — kept for comparison as a highly relevant infrastructure benchmark for the Dutch / EU open banking model.

PSD2 + iDEAL
Ra
Rabobank
Traditional Dutch Bank · ASPSP + Business Payment
VRP ✗ BNPL ✗ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Traditional Dutch bank with a strong PSD2 / business payments infrastructure and a developer ecosystem for payments, data and TPP connectivity. Public API stack emphasises payment initiation, business bulk flows, instant payouts and consent-based access.
Target Segment
Primarily businesses, direct customers, TPPs and finance teams using Rabobank business accounts. Single/bulk credit transfers, direct debits and instant payouts for business use cases.
Service Role
ASPSP + account provider plus business payment API provider. Rabobank is the bank that holds the account and executes consented payment initiation and business payment flows.
Bulk Payments PSD2 Business API
Strengths
Strongest in Bulk Payments and business payment infrastructure. Rabobank explicitly supports Business Bulk Payment Initiation and also offers a fully automated Business Instant Payout API for trigger-based payouts without manual approval. Strong benchmark for batch and payout operations.
Limitations
In public open banking materials, Rabobank is less visible as a native VRP or embedded BNPL benchmark. Public proposition is much clearer around PSD2/business payment initiation, bulk flows and payout automation.
Key Conclusion
Best Dutch benchmark for PSD2-based bulk payments, business accounts, payout infrastructure and consented business payment flows. Useful if case needs a bank-side enterprise payment model.
Ya
Yapily
Open Banking Provider · API Orchestration (not a bank)
VRP ✓ BNPL ~ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Open banking infrastructure provider operating across Europe. Positions itself as a payments-and-data platform with products for Payments, Data, VRP, Commercial VRP, Bulk Payments and regulated access through Yapily Connect.
Target Segment
Fintechs, lenders, PSPs, merchants, platforms, banking/EMI players and businesses needing one API for data and payments. In the Netherlands, Yapily explicitly targets Dutch SME use cases such as bulk payments, onboarding and reconciliation.
Service Role
Open banking infrastructure and orchestration layer rather than a bank or lender. Enables AISP/PISP-style connectivity, gives one API for data and payment initiation.
VRP + cVRP Bulk Payments BNPL Enablement
Strengths
Strongest in VRP and open banking payment orchestration. Yapily offers VRP and cVRP products, supports customer-defined limits, one-time authorisation, revocable mandates and reusable payment logic. Also strong in Bulk Payments and BNPL enablement through open banking data for instant verification in lending journeys.
Limitations
Yapily is not a bank and not a lender, so for BNPL it is an enablement layer rather than the credit product itself. Documented VRP scope is currently centred on Sweeping VRP; broader commercial recurring use cases are presented separately as cVRP.
Key Conclusion
Best Dutch/EU benchmark for VRP-like orchestration, pay-by-bank infrastructure, payment/data APIs and BNPL enablement through open banking data. Useful if case needs a provider-side model rather than a bank-only view.
🇳🇱
Netherlands Country Conclusion

The clearest split is this: Rabobank is the better benchmark for bank-side bulk and business payment flows, while Yapily is the better benchmark for VRP-like orchestration, API enablement and broader open banking service design. BNPL in this market is more convincingly understood through provider-enabled lending journeys than through a native bank product benchmark.

Market Profile — Australia
🇦🇺

Australia

In Australia, Open Banking sits under the Consumer Data Right (CDR) and is primarily a data-sharing framework. Banking was the first sector under CDR; non-bank lenders start from 2026, and there are currently no action declarations or action initiation rules in force. The closest practical analogue to VRP is PayTo and related recurring-payment infrastructure, not CDR itself.

Consumer Data Right (CDR)
CB
CommBank
Major Australian Bank · CDR Data Holder + Merchant Payments
VRP ~ BNPL ✓ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Major Australian bank participating in CDR/Open Banking as a data holder, with customer-facing data-sharing controls in the CommBank app / NetBank, plus a broader payments stack for business and institutional clients.
Target Segment
Split between retail customers for StepPay and PayTo management, and business/corporate clients for CommBiz, payment file imports, PayTo API and transaction banking.
Service Role
Account provider / data holder under CDR, merchant and business payment provider through CommBiz and institutional APIs, and lender via StepPay.
StepPay (BNPL) CDR Data Holder CommBiz Bulk
Strengths
Strongest in BNPL and solid in business payments. StepPay is a clear BNPL-style product — split $100+ purchases into four equal repayments with no interest or monthly fees. CommBank is also strong in VRP-like recurring payments via PayTo (authorise, pause, resume or cancel agreements) and bulk payments through CommBiz file imports and ISO 20022.
Limitations
Open Banking layer is still tied to Australia's read-access CDR model, not live payment initiation under CDR. BNPL and payment stack are strong, but less explicitly packaged as a unified developer-led recurring-payments platform than Westpac's QuickStream materials.
Key Conclusion
Best Australian benchmark for BNPL and a useful bank-side benchmark for PayTo-enabled recurring payments plus business payment file flows.
We
Westpac
Major Australian Bank · CDR Holder + ADR + Business Payments
VRP ✓ BNPL ~ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Major Australian bank positioned in CDR/Open Banking as both a data holder and accredited data recipient, with a strong overlay of business payments and merchant infrastructure through QuickStream.
Target Segment
Covers retail customers for PayTo and PartPay, and merchants, businesses, finance teams and corporates through QuickStream, PayTo for corporates and file-based payment tools.
Service Role
Account provider / data holder and ADR under CDR, payment orchestration provider through QuickStream and PayTo APIs, and lender through PartPay / instalment-style credit features.
PayTo (VRP-like) QuickStream Bulk PartPay
Strengths
Strongest in VRP-like recurring payments and bulk/business payment infrastructure. Westpac's PayTo and QuickStream stack supports recurring payment schedules, PayTo Agreements APIs and merchant receivables, while Westpac Online Banking and QuickStream support ABA/CSV/payment file uploads, multiple payments and payment-file APIs.
Limitations
Westpac's BNPL-like proposition is less native than CommBank's StepPay because PartPay is a credit-card feature, not a standalone BNPL account. Like CommBank, its Open Banking capability under CDR is still fundamentally about data sharing, not action initiation.
Key Conclusion
Stronger Australian benchmark for VRP-like recurring payment orchestration and bulk/business payments, especially where merchant collections, recurring agreements and payment-file infrastructure matter.
🇦🇺
Australia Country Conclusion

Australia is useful less as a benchmark for native Open Banking payments and more as a benchmark for how banks combine CDR data sharing with adjacent payment rails like PayTo, business payment files and bank-owned instalment products. For your case, CommBank is the stronger benchmark for BNPL, while Westpac is the stronger benchmark for VRP-like recurring flows and bulk payments.

Market Profile — Brazil
🇧🇷

Brazil

In Brazil, the most relevant market lens is Open Finance + Pix, not PSD2-style Open Banking. The closest practical analogue to VRP is Pix Automático — a recurring-payment mechanism where the payer authorises once and payments then happen automatically for subscriptions, utilities, schools, gyms and similar recurring bills.

Open Finance + Pix Automático
Nu
Nubank
Digital-first Bank · Consumer Open Finance + BNPL
VRP ~ BNPL ✓ Bulk ✗
OB/OF Positioning
Digital-first financial platform with strong consumer-facing Open Finance adoption. Highlights Open Finance benefits such as external-data consents, account aggregation and customer savings/alerts, while packaging payments into app-native experiences.
Target Segment
Primarily retail customers for NuPay and in-app payment journeys, plus small businesses / merchants through Nu Empresas collection tools.
Service Role
Consumer-facing payment provider and lender-like enabler in checkout flows through NuPay, plus a merchant collections provider through Nu Empresas tools such as cobrança, recurring charges and payment management.
NuPay BNPL Open Finance Pix Automático
Strengths
Strongest in BNPL and strong in user-controlled recurring payments. NuPay supports online checkout directly in the app, can provide additional temporary limit and allows instalments of up to 24 times. For recurring/VRP-like logic, Nubank offers Pix Automático inside its payment assistant, with alerts, payment control and configurable limits.
Limitations
Public evidence is much weaker for enterprise-grade bulk outgoing payments. Public business materials are more clearly focused on collections, recurring charges, boletos, Pix and merchant cash-in tools than on large-scale batch payout infrastructure.
Key Conclusion
Best Brazilian benchmark for BNPL, consumer checkout and customer-controlled payment journeys. Useful if case focuses on user experience, app-native payment confirmation and flexible recurring payments with strong customer control.
It
Itaú Unibanco
Universal Bank · Open Finance + Pix Infrastructure
VRP ✓ BNPL ~ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Universal bank with a broader Open Finance + payments infrastructure approach. Combines Open Finance features with Pix, including payment initiation using balances from other banks, and positions Pix/Open Finance as part of a wider business-payments ecosystem.
Target Segment
Covers both retail and business customers, but especially strong for companies, suppliers, finance teams and SMEs through Itaú Empresas, Pix PJ and SISPAG.
Service Role
Account provider, Open Finance payment initiator, business payment orchestrator and lender in parcelled Pix / working-capital-type flows. Also exposes a developer/open-finance layer and business payment stack.
Pix Automático (VRP) SISPAG / CNAB Pague Parcelado
Strengths
Strongest in VRP-like recurring payments and Bulk Payments. Itaú Empresas offers Pix Automático for recurring payments and subscriptions, supports Open Finance payment initiation with balances from other banks, and has clear bulk/business infrastructure through SISPAG, CNAB file flows and payroll/supplier batch payments.
Limitations
BNPL is present, but looks more like a traditional bank credit / parcelled Pix logic than a truly embedded, checkout-native BNPL ecosystem. Its strongest public signal is infrastructure and business payments, not consumer-led BNPL UX.
Key Conclusion
Best Brazilian benchmark for VRP-like recurring payments in a business context and for Bulk Payments. Useful if case focuses on merchant-side recurring collections, payment orchestration, payout infrastructure, payroll or supplier flows.
🇧🇷
Brazil Country Conclusion

Brazil is one of the most relevant markets for this case because it shows how Open Finance and Pix can be combined into real payment products. Nubank is the better benchmark for BNPL and consumer UX, while Itaú is the better benchmark for VRP-like recurring business payments and bulk payment infrastructure. In Brazilian market logic, Pix Automático should be treated as the closest practical equivalent to VRP.

Market Profile — France
🇫🇷

France

France is a strong benchmark for bank-led payments, merchant acceptance and BNPL, but a weaker benchmark for native VRP logic than the UK. The clearest French patterns are PSD2/Open Banking account-to-account payments, SEPA Request-to-Pay / instant payments, and bank-backed BNPL — rather than a distinct, UK-style VRP product layer.

PSD2 + SEPA R2P + BNPL
BN
BNP Paribas
Large Incumbent · Merchant Payments + BNPL (FLOA/Instanea)
VRP ✗ BNPL ✓ Bulk ~
OB/OF Positioning
Large incumbent bank with a clear merchant-payments and Open Banking posture. BNP Paribas publicly presents Instanea as an online payment service combining Open Banking APIs and instant SEPA transfers, and positions itself in payments innovation through Request-to-Pay and BNPL.
Target Segment
Primarily merchants, corporates and treasurers for payments and receivables, plus consumers and merchants for BNPL through FLOA. Materials explicitly position Instanea for merchants and FLOA for split payments and mini-loans.
Service Role
Account provider + merchant payment enabler + lender. BNP acts as a bank-side payment and collections provider, an Open Banking A2A enabler through Instanea, and a lender through FLOA / BNPL solutions.
FLOA / BNPL Instanea A2A Bulk Transfers
Strengths
Strongest in BNPL and strong in merchant-facing Open Banking payments. BNP owns FLOA, which it calls a major player and French leader in split payments/BNPL, and offers Instanea for A2A merchant checkout. Also shows strong bulk/business payment capability through bulk-file transmission, XML specifications and corporate cash-management tooling.
Limitations
BNP Paribas is less explicit as a native VRP benchmark. Public French/EU payment story is clearer around Open Banking A2A checkout, Request-to-Pay, instant payments and BNPL than around a dedicated VRP layer like in the UK.
Key Conclusion
Best French benchmark for BNPL, merchant checkout and bank-led Open Banking payment acceptance, with solid additional relevance for corporate/bulk payment infrastructure.
SG
Société Générale
Wholesale-led Incumbent · API + Corporate Payments
VRP ✗ BNPL ~ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
Large incumbent bank with a strong API- and wholesale-led Open Banking posture. Société Générale says Open Banking has enabled it to offer broader services through the SG MARKETS portal, with 2,000+ APIs in production in its Global Banking and Investor Solutions activities.
Target Segment
Primarily wholesale clients, SMEs, multinationals, large corporates and merchants. SG's wholesale materials emphasise corporate API services, payment automation and ISO 20022-payment-type support for business customers.
Service Role
Account provider + API / service distributor + merchant payment provider + lender. SG provides payment services for e-commerce and offers card-linked credit through an Open Banking and APIs approach.
2,000+ APIs ISO 20022 Bulk PSD2 / R2P
Strengths
Strongest in API-led corporate payments, merchant acceptance and business payment infrastructure. SG highlights Open Banking for corporates, payment automation, multi-bank connectivity and ISO 20022/payment-type support; it also positions Request-to-Pay and instant payments as important building blocks for collections and payment journeys.
Limitations
Société Générale is also weaker as a native VRP benchmark, and its BNPL signal is less product-native than BNP's. The public offer looks closer to merchant acceptance, payment initiation, instant payments, Request-to-Pay and traditional card-linked credit than to a clear standalone BNPL or UK-style VRP proposition.
Key Conclusion
Best French benchmark for API-led corporate payments, merchant acceptance and business payment orchestration, but less strong than BNP Paribas for a clear BNPL benchmark.
🇫🇷
France Country Conclusion

France is best understood as a market where banks package PSD2/Open Banking, merchant payments, instant payments and bank-backed BNPL into practical payment propositions. BNP Paribas is the better benchmark for BNPL and merchant checkout, while Société Générale is the better benchmark for API-led corporate payments and payment orchestration. France is less convincing than the UK for native VRP, and stronger for bank-driven payment acceptance, collections and corporate payment flows.

Market Profile — Germany
🇩🇪

Germany

Germany is a strong benchmark for PSD2 / Berlin Group infrastructure, but a weaker benchmark for native VRP logic than the UK. The German open-banking model is centred on AIS and PIS under PSD2, and Berlin Group's XS2A framework is one of the main technical standards used across Europe. In practice, this makes Germany stronger for regulated account access, payment initiation and infrastructure-led services than for a distinct bank-native VRP layer.

PSD2 / Berlin Group XS2A
So
Solaris SE
BaaS Bank · Embedded Finance + BNPL API Platform
VRP ~ BNPL ✓ Bulk ✓
OB/OF Positioning
API-first Banking-as-a-Service bank with one of the clearest embedded-finance positions in Germany. Solaris presents itself as a German-licensed bank offering digital banking, payments, lending and BNPL through APIs.
Target Segment
Partners, fintechs, merchants, digital banks, SaaS platforms, consumers, freelancers and businesses that want to embed accounts, payments or lending. Solaris explicitly markets services for consumers, freelancers and business users.
Service Role
Licensed bank + infrastructure provider + lender. Solaris can act as an account provider, platform, bulk-payment infrastructure and lending enabler through APIs.
Splitpay (BNPL) SEPA Credit Batch BaaS API
Strengths
Strongest in BNPL and Bulk Payments. Solaris has a clear BNPL product, Splitpay — a revolving-credit-based buy-now-pay-later model. It offers SEPA Credit Batch Transfers plus payout infrastructure through its payments stack. Also supports timed orders, relevant for scheduled payment logic.
Limitations
Solaris is not a native PSD2/VRP bank benchmark in the UK sense. Its strongest recurring-payment logic is infrastructure-led and API-driven, but public materials point more clearly to scheduled payments, batch transfers and BNPL than to a dedicated bank-native VRP product.
Key Conclusion
Best German benchmark for embedded finance, API-led payments, BNPL enablement and batch payment infrastructure. Useful if case needs a Germany-based example of how banking capabilities are packaged for partners and platforms.
Co
Comdirect
Retail Digital Bank · PSD2 Compliance + REST API
VRP ✗ BNPL ✗ Bulk ✗
OB/OF Positioning
Retail-first direct bank brand of Commerzbank with a PSD2-compliant XS2A / Open Banking layer and its own broader REST API for banking and brokerage. Public positioning is closer to open banking compliance + retail digital banking than to embedded-finance infrastructure.
Target Segment
Primarily retail customers, investors and API users who want access to their own accounts, cards, depots and brokerage functionality; PSD2 APIs also support regulated TPP access.
Service Role
ASPSP / retail account provider with PSD2 APIs and a proprietary REST API; in lending scenarios, acts more like a traditional card- or loan-based provider than an embedded-finance platform.
PSD2 / XS2A REST API
Strengths
Strongest in regulated Open Banking access and retail digital API coverage. Comdirect provides live AIS, PIIS and PIS APIs under PSD2, and its proprietary REST API goes further by supporting balances, transactions, documents and brokerage orders.
Limitations
Comdirect is much weaker in your selected-service scope. Public evidence is limited for native VRP, enterprise bulk payments and a strong embedded BNPL proposition. Its instalment logic is closer to a card-based 3-instalment feature and standard loans than to a full BNPL or recurring-payments model.
Key Conclusion
Better treated as a benchmark for retail PSD2 / open-banking compliance and open-brokerage evolution, not as a leading benchmark for VRP, BNPL or bulk-payment innovation.
🇩🇪
Germany Country Conclusion

Germany is best understood as a market for PSD2 / Berlin Group infrastructure, not for bank-native VRP innovation. For your case, Solaris SE is the stronger benchmark for BNPL and bulk/API-led payment infrastructure, while comdirect is mainly useful as a reference for retail PSD2 access and compliance-oriented open banking. Germany is stronger for infrastructure and API enablement than for a clear UK-style VRP benchmark.
